Buy-Sell Matching & Filing Strategy

Focused training for FRT account managers supporting asset owners and public pensions

Introduction

Settlements in securities class actions use a Plan of Allocation to distribute funds based on Recognized Loss. This training focuses on buy-sell matching and the filing strategy for asset owner clients, especially public pensions.

Core Focus: Aggregate vs. Disaggregate filing decisions and how FIFO matching drives Recognized Loss.

Aggregate vs. Disaggregate Filing

For asset owner clients (e.g., public pensions), FRT compares Aggregate filing vs. Disaggregate filing to maximize Recognized Loss.

Aggregate Filing

Account Name ClientShortname AGGREGATE

  • We submit one consolidated claim for the client.
  • Used when Aggregate analysis yields higher Recognized Loss.
  • Sometimes yields more RL when positions net favorably across accounts.

Disaggregate Filing

  • Used when Aggregate RL is smaller than filing accounts separately.
  • We run transfers, then file each eligible account individually.
  • Maximizes RL in cases where individual account outcomes are stronger.
  • We do not use Aggregate filing for Fair Fund cases because doc audits are too complicated at the aggregate level.
Decision Rule: If Aggregate RL < Disaggregate RL, we file each eligible account individually. If Aggregate RL is higher, we file one consolidated claim.

Buy-Sell Matching Methods

Matching determines which purchases are paired with sales for Recognized Loss calculations.

  • FIFO: Matches oldest buys to sales. Standard in class actions and typically maximizes losses.
  • LIFO: Matches newest buys to sales. Rare in litigation.
  • Specific Identification: Precise lot matching. Complex for large portfolios.

How to Explain Matching to Clients

Simple framing: “Matching is the rule that determines which purchases are paired with which sales to calculate loss.”

  • FIFO (standard in settlements): oldest buys matched first, which often increases recognized loss.
  • Why it matters: matching can change recognized loss even if total shares are the same.
  • What clients care about: consistency with the Plan of Allocation and fairness across all claimants.

Step-by-Step Demonstration (High-School Friendly)

Think of it like a stack of boxes: you bought two boxes of shares at different prices, and later you sold one box.

Buy #2 500 shares at $12 (newer)
Sell 1,000 shares at $10

FIFO Result

  1. FIFO means “first in, first out.”
  2. You sell the oldest box first: $20 → $10.
  3. Loss per share = $20 - $10 = $10.
  4. Total RL = 1,000 × $10 = $10,000.

LIFO Result

  1. LIFO means “last in, first out.”
  2. You sell the newest box first: $12 → $10.
  3. Loss = (500 × $2) + (500 × $10) = $6,000.
Why this matters: Same trades, different method, different loss.

Filing Lifecycle: Complaint to Disbursement

This end-to-end lifecycle reflects how cases move from initial complaints through settlement, filing, and disbursement.

  1. Initial Court Complaints Filed: Multiple complaints are filed in federal court after the alleged disclosure event.
  2. Consolidation & Lead Plaintiff Motion: Related complaints are consolidated; investors apply to be lead plaintiff.
  3. Appointment of Lead Plaintiff & Counsel: Court appoints lead plaintiff and approves lead counsel.
  4. Class Certification & Litigation: Motions, discovery, and rulings shape the case posture.
  5. Settlement Negotiation: Parties reach a settlement agreement and propose a Plan of Allocation.
  6. Preliminary Approval & Notice: Court grants preliminary approval and notices are sent.
  7. Claim Filing Window: Investors submit claims with trade data and supporting documentation.
  8. Filing Document Audits: Claims are reviewed, reconciled, and audited for accuracy and eligibility.
  9. Final Approval: Court grants final approval of the settlement and allocation plan.
  10. Disbursement: Approved claims are paid out pro-rata based on Recognized Loss.

Opt-in vs. Opt-out Participation

US: Opt-out System

  • Eligible investors are automatically included unless they opt out.
  • Maximizes participation and total recovery pool.

EU/Other: Opt-in Systems

  • Investors must actively opt in to participate.
  • Requires proactive client outreach and enrollment.

Lead Plaintiff Selection

Large institutional investors can seek lead plaintiff status to influence strategy, settlement terms, and counsel selection.

  • Selection typically favors the investor with the largest financial interest.
  • Lead plaintiff has a fiduciary role to protect the class.
  • For large asset owners, this can improve outcomes and visibility.
